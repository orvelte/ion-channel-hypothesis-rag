Selective block of SCN9A promises to produce non-narcotic analgesic activity without motor or cognitive impairment. Several SCN9A-selective blockers have been reported, but efficacy in animal pain models required high multiples of the IC50 for channel block. text: Studies using heterologous expression and functional analysis of recombinant SCN1A channels suggest that epilepsy mutations in SCN1A may cause either gain-of-function or loss-of-function effects that are consistent with either increased or decreased neuronal excitability. How these diverse effects lead to epilepsy is poorly understood. This review summarizes the data on sodium channel mutations and epilepsy and builds a case for the hypothesis that most SCN1A mutations have their ultimate epileptogenic effects by reducing SCN1A-mediated whole cell sodium currents in GABAergic neurons, resulting in widespread loss of brain inhibition, an ideal background for the genesis of epileptic seizures.

text: Electrophysiological studies indicate that Nae1 deletion reduced PVIN excitability and GABA release and consequently increased the network excitability of pyramidal neurons (PyNs). Further analysis revealed a reduction in sodium-current density, not a change in channel property, in mutant PVINs and decreased SCN1A protein levels. These results suggest that insufficient neddylation in PVINs reduces SCN1A stability and thus the excitability of PVINs; the ensuing increased PyN activity causes seizures in mice. Consistently, SCN1A was found reduced by proteomic analysis that revealed abnormality in synapses and metabolic pathways. Our findings describe a role of neddylation in maintaining SCN1A stability for PVIN excitability and reveal what we believe is a new mechanism in the pathogenesis of epilepsy.
